# Bird-Dogging: Overview

## What it is

In project management, **bird-dogging** is the practice of actively tracking and chasing down a task, issue, or deliverable to make sure it gets completed. The bird-dogger isn't necessarily the person doing the work — they're the person making sure the work doesn't fall through the cracks.

The term comes from hunting: bird dogs locate, flush, and retrieve game for the hunter. In project work, the bird-dogger does the same for action items, blockers, and dependencies.

## Core responsibilities

- **Follow up persistently** on open action items, decisions, and blockers
- **Own the chase, not the work** — accountability for driving to resolution, not for execution
- **Escalate** when items stall or owners are unresponsive
- **Close the loop** by confirming completion and reporting status back to stakeholders

## When it's useful

- Cross-functional work where dependencies sit with other teams
- Long-running initiatives with many small open threads
- Recovery situations where commitments have slipped
- Anytime there's risk of an item being forgotten between status meetings

## What it is not

- **Not micromanagement** — the bird-dogger trusts the owner to do the work, but verifies it lands
- **Not doing the work yourself** — if you take it over, you've stopped bird-dogging
- **Not a one-time nudge** — it's sustained follow-through until the item is closed

## Typical cadence

1. Identify the open item and its owner
2. Confirm the expected completion date
3. Check in at appropriate intervals (before the due date, on it, and after if needed)
4. Escalate if blocked or slipping
5. Verify completion and mark closed
